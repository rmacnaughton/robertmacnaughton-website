## Robert MacNaughton — Website Positioning Context

### Purpose of this document

This is the strategic constraint document for RobertMacNaughton.com.
All website copy should be written from this document — not by copying it, but by internalizing it.

This reflects the real person, the real work, the real voice. It was developed through extended conversation with Robert, and draws directly from how he actually describes what he does.

---

## How the Site Functions

The site is a brochure — a credibility artifact. Most visitors arrive because they met Robert, were referred to him, or encountered him somewhere. The site confirms what they already sensed and makes it easy to reach out.

It should serve:
- The person ready to book a call today
- The person who emails directly
- The person who checks the site and comes back in six months

It should not try to convert, persuade, or funnel anyone. It should feel like access, not advertisement.

**Design principle:**
Clear enough to land. Rich enough to make someone curious. Never dumbed down.

The site should make smart people lean in — not feel like they're being handled.

## What Robert Actually Does

Here's how Robert describes it:

**The core work:** He quickly assesses a leader's native capabilities — what got them their success — then develops a leadership plan targeting the high-leverage growth areas they need to measure and manage. All along, he's teaching normative best practices, systems for managing growth, and modeling how to have effective meetings and relational conversations.

**Difficult conversations are the center of gravity.** Over half of what Robert helps leaders do is strategize difficult conversations: models for how to have them successfully, understanding the invisible power dynamics at play, the mindset to hold so they're not just trying to prove they're right (or at least noticing when they are). His core conviction: *the cost of not having the conversation is the most costly thing in your life. But if you set the conversation up for success, it goes well much more often than people expect. We can create a game we can actually win.*

**He does the practical stuff too.** He'll make a client share their screen and send the email they've been avoiding for two weeks. They kick and scream, then feel so much better — and often ask him to do it again. He audits calendars. He integrates PM systems. He coaches executive assistants. He holds accountability for existential co-founder conversations and for simple tasks alike. Don't position him away from accountability or performance coaching — that's often exactly what he's doing, and it's what gets people in the door.

**The deeper work comes when trust is earned.** Robert meets people where they are. Early work often looks like strategic thinking, decision support, and practical traction. That's real and valuable on its own. But it's also the doorway. For his more long-term clients, he's deep into their lives — health strategy, marriage issues, identity transformation, whatever is at the root of their stress — because that's where the outsized impact on leadership actually lives. The deeper psychological, relational, and developmental shifts happen when the relationship has earned it.

**The nervous system piece.** Robert's presence itself is part of the intervention. He allows his clients' nervous systems to entrain to his — walking their state back toward ground where they can relax, think clearly, and access perspective they wouldn't have alone. He calls it "golden eyes" (borrowed from his teachers): seeing every person as though they're more evolved than he is, and helping them notice anywhere they're playing small. His state says "of course you can do this," so they can begin to believe it too.

**Assessments and 360 work.** Robert leads with cutting-edge assessment instruments (evolving practically every time he uses them) and thorough stakeholder interviews — co-founders, investors, direct reports, spouses, therapists, past colleagues. Anyone who helps him understand the leader better. He gets as much surface area as possible to enable optimized contribution.

**Team and organizational work.** Most work is 1:1, but Robert regularly mediates co-founder misalignments and facilitates leadership team issues: strategy, tactics, relational/cultural dynamics, and systems.

## Key Frames and Talk Tracks

These are Robert's own frames. They can be referenced, adapted, or quoted on the site:

**Motorboat → Sailboat.** Many leaders got where they are by knowing how to motor hard — white-knuckling things into existence. That's scarcity-based motivation. The motorboat can go fast in any direction, but it takes a lot of fuel and it's not a comfortable ride. The sailboat, by contrast, reads the winds — and if the craft is right, you can sail downwind at 50 knots having a quiet conversation. Sailboat leadership happens when leaders recognize that silence isn't a signal of anxiety but the prerequisite for listening, insight, and inspiration. It allows much bigger risk-taking. It can take years to stabilize, but it's where leaders need to grow to meet the anxiety-ridden polycrisis we're facing.

**The cost of delay.** The number one impact to a company's bottom line is the time between when a decision has been made and when you do something about it. Similarly: the time between when you recognize a mistake and when you fix it. Start a 90-day clock. Put it on your calendar. Identify expectations, measure progress, give every opportunity to improve — and know when you have to have the hard conversation. Transition costs are high, but nowhere near as high as the cost of waiting too long to do what you knew you needed to do earlier.

**The cost of the conversation you're not having.** The most expensive thing in your life is the conversation you're avoiding. But set it up for success, and it goes well far more often than people anticipate. We can create a game we can actually win.

## Tone

**Sound like Robert.** Direct, fast, precise, warm, intellectually confident, self-effacing, funny when appropriate, unafraid of complexity.

* Big words are fine when they're the right words. Don't dumb things down.
* Trust the reader's intelligence.
* Warmth comes through as taking someone seriously, not trying to make them comfortable.
* Let some things be provocative or counterintuitive.
* Coaching is never what people imagine it will be. The copy can reflect that.

**Avoid:**
* Coaching clichés ("unlock your potential," "10x your leadership," "thrive in disruption")
* Copy that could appear on any coach's website
* Explaining what coaching is to people who already know they need it
* Being precious about categories — Robert does accountability work, strategic work, psychological work, practical work, and deep developmental work. The site should hold all of it.
